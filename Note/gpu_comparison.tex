    \subsection{Decomposition of Execution Time and I/O Analysis}% (fold)
    \label{sub:decomposition_of_execution_time_and_i_o_analysis}

        For our lightweight Dense-NN tasks (comprising approximately 5\,MB of weights and batch data per iteration), the total execution time is heavily influenced by data movement rather than pure theoretical TFLOPS. The execution time can be decomposed into a four-stage pipeline:
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{CPU to VRAM:} Data shuffling, preprocessing, and dispatching via the PCIe bus. Limited by Host CPU frequency.
            \item \textbf{VRAM to L2 Cache:} Moving data from VRAM (GDDR6/HBM2) into the L2 Cache. Limited by memory bandwidth, latency, and L2 cache capacity.
            \item \textbf{L2 Cache to Core:} Moving data from L2 cache to the L1 cache/Registers for calculation.
            \item \textbf{Core Computation:} The actual matrix multiplications in the Tensor Cores. Limited strictly by the GPU's clock speed.
        \end{enumerate}

    \subsection{Key Observations}% (fold)
    \label{sub:key_observations}

        Based on the benchmark results in Table~\ref{tab:gpu_performance} and the hardware specifications, our observations are as follows:

        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{The L2 Cache Advantage and Clock Speed Dominance (L40S \& RTX 4090):} 
            The Ada Lovelace cards deliver the best performance, completing the training in nearly one-third the time of the RTX A6000. Due to their massive L2 caches, these GPUs bypass the GDDR6 latency. With data readily available on-chip, the performance is bottlenecked solely by the core computation.

            \item \textbf{Clock Speed Ceiling on High-Bandwidth Flagships (A100 \& V100):} 
            The A100 and V100 yield practically identical execution times. Despite the V100 having only 6\,MB of L2 cache, its HBM2 memory provides ultra-low physical latency, easily saturating the I/O requirements for this small workload. The A100's larger L2 cache and extreme HBM2e bandwidth offer no additional benefit here, as the V100 is already not I/O-bound. Consequently, the bottleneck shifts to computation. Since both cards share similar core clock speeds, their training durations naturally tie.

            \item \textbf{Internal I/O Bottleneck and GDDR Latency (RTX A6000):} 
            The RTX A6000 performs the slowest despite having a higher clock speed than the A100. Due to its minimal 6\,MB L2 cache and  GDDR6 memory, the A6000 suffers from severe latency during Stage 2 (VRAM-to-L2 transfers). The computing cores are frequently starved, waiting for data delivery, rendering its theoretical compute capabilities unachievable.

            \item \textbf{Host CPU Bottlenecks on Identical Hardware (RTX A6000 baseline):} 
            Comparing the RTX A6000 between our Local Server (\texttt{pheno-2}) and the NCTS Cluster (\texttt{gpuserver03}), NCTS shows a 10--15\% speed advantage. This is primarily a Stage 1 bottleneck. The NCTS node utilizes a Xeon Gold 6226R at a higher base clock of 2.90\,GHz compared to our local 2.10\,GHz CPU. This 38\% increase in host CPU frequency accelerates the initial data-feeding and kernel launch processes.
        \end{itemize}
